How I Built a Lifecycle Marketing Program from Scratch
I came into my current role as a web developer. Over the next year and a half, the work expanded into content, then newsletters, then customer-facing tools, and eventually into building out the entire technical side of marketing. By the time I'd been there a year, I was the one-person marketing tech function at the company, designing and shipping the lifecycle infrastructure that the marketing team runs on.
This post is about that infrastructure. Specifically, the lifecycle program: how leads come in, how they get routed across systems, how they're nurtured toward demos, how engagement gets segmented into tiers, and how everything transitions when someone converts to a customer. If you're working in lifecycle marketing, or if you're the technical person at a small company who's been handed the marketing problem because there's nobody else to do it, hopefully some of this is useful.
Numbers for context: the program runs on a list of around 100,000 subscribers including 7,000+ active customers. It delivered 187 campaigns last year. About 25,000 contacts have engaged in the last 90 days. The whole thing runs without manual intervention.
The Setup
Before I designed the lifecycle program, the systems weren't talking to each other. The marketing platform didn't know what the CRM knew. The CRM didn't know what people had clicked. The booking tool didn't push data anywhere automatically. Sales was working out of one set of tools, marketing out of another, and the connection between them was a person manually updating spreadsheets when they had time.
This is a really common situation at small B2B companies. You can't justify hiring a dedicated marketing operations specialist until the business is bigger, but you can't grow the business as fast without lifecycle infrastructure. So whoever is technical enough to wire the systems together gets handed the problem.
The first job was just to install the connections. The harder job was designing them so they actually fit together and could scale.
The Architecture
The system has five flows that work together. I'll walk through each one.
1. Lead Capture
Leads come in from three sources: webinar registrations, homepage forms, and Calendly bookings. Each source captures different data, but all of it gets normalized and tagged with a primary audience, the original source, and any UTM parameters from the campaign that drove them in. That tagging matters because it determines what they see for the rest of their lifecycle.
The webinar funnel is the most layered. Someone hits a landing page, fills out a form that captures email, name, role, and organization, and that data gets pushed in real time to both the marketing platform and the CRM via API. Based on their role and org, they get tagged into segments and dropped into different downstream automations. After submission, they go through a progressive profiling step using an embedded form that prefills their email and name and asks a few additional qualification questions. Then they hit a final slide with a button to add the webinar to their calendar.
The reason I built it this way is that progressive profiling lets you ask for more data without scaring off the initial signup. People will give you their email and name to register. They will not give you their email, name, role, organization size, budget, and timeline at the same moment. So you collect the minimum to convert them, then enrich after.
2. Demo Booking
When someone books a demo, a webhook fires that does several things at once. It extracts the booking details (email, name, assigned rep, meeting time, UTM data), updates the contact in the marketing platform with a "demo booked" flag and date, sends a confirmation email, and either creates a new CRM contact or updates an existing one.
The "or updates an existing one" part is where most implementations get sloppy. If the contact already exists in the CRM with an original lead source and assigned rep, you do not want to overwrite that data when they book a demo. They might already be owned by a different sales rep. Their original source might be from a campaign that's tracking attribution. The right behavior is to append to the contact's history (a summary line saying "demo booked with X on Y") without touching ownership, source, or status.
This sounds obvious in retrospect but it's a common bug in CRM integrations. A lot of implementations just upsert blindly and overwrite real attribution data, which makes downstream reporting useless.
3. Demo Nurture
If someone is a lead but hasn't booked a demo, they get enrolled in a five-week sequence of "book a demo" reminder emails. The sequence stops immediately if they book. They're excluded from other tier-based emails while in the sequence so they're not getting blasted with multiple campaigns at once.
I added a behavioral trigger on top of that: anyone who clicks a "book a demo" link in any of our emails but doesn't actually book within a window gets pulled into a focused recovery sequence. The reasoning was that clicking the link is high intent, much higher than just opening an email. Recovering even a fraction of those clicks into actual bookings is meaningful pipeline.
After implementing the trigger, demo bookings went up. The lift wasn't massive in absolute numbers, but the effort to build it was small and the recovered leads were essentially free compared to any other source.
4. Tiered Engagement
Once someone finishes the demo nurture or books a demo, they get sorted into one of three engagement tiers based on what they actually do.
Tier 1 is the most engaged: people who booked a demo, or people who finished demo nurture and clicked something in the last 90 days. They get educational content and product-focused emails.
Tier 2 is moderately engaged: opened recently but didn't click. They get lighter, more curiosity-driven content.
Tier 3 is re-engagement territory: no opens in 90+ days. They get a different kind of campaign focused on getting them to come back, or eventually a sunset sequence.
Tier membership isn't static. Someone in Tier 1 who goes quiet for 90 days drops to Tier 3. Someone in Tier 3 who suddenly clicks a link bumps back up. The whole point is to match the cadence and content to the actual engagement level rather than carpet-bombing everyone equally.
5. Customer Lifecycle
Once a lead becomes a customer, they exit the lead nurture system entirely. They don't get demo nurture. They don't get tiered marketing emails. Instead they enter a renewal flow that fires a check-in six months before their renewal date and a reminder one month before.
This separation matters because the messaging for "I'm trying to get you to buy" is fundamentally different from "you already bought, please renew." Mixing them confuses customers and burns goodwill.
How I Actually Built This
I'm not a traditional engineer. I came into this work as a web developer, but I've increasingly become an architect and operator: I design the systems, make the decisions about what should happen and how, and use AI tooling extensively for implementation.
What that looks like in practice: I figure out the architecture (what flows need to exist, what data has to move where, what the edge cases are, what the integrations should look like). I write specs and Figma diagrams that capture the logic. Then I work with AI tools to implement specific pieces (webhook handlers, API request scaffolding, template variations, debugging when things break). The design decisions and the integration patterns are mine. The boilerplate code is largely AI-accelerated under my direction.
I think this is going to be a much more common way to work in marketing engineering and adjacent roles over the next few years. The bottleneck on building this kind of infrastructure isn't typing speed. It's having the judgment to know what to build, in what order, with what edge cases handled. AI tools are great at the implementation. They can't tell you what the right architecture is for your specific business.
For this project, that meant I could ship something at a pace that would normally require a small engineering team. A one-person marketing tech function at a small company is only possible because of this kind of leverage.
What I'd Do Differently
A few things I'd build into v2:
The renewal flow has a gap around customers who don't renew. That's currently handled manually, which means it sometimes doesn't happen as cleanly as it should. A proper churn flow would trigger automatically and route those contacts into a different program rather than waiting for someone to notice.
Canceled and rescheduled demos aren't handled gracefully. If someone reschedules, the original demo nurture should re-pause cleanly, and the new booking should fire a fresh confirmation. Right now this is brittle.
The tier transition logic could be smarter. The current rules are pretty static (clicked or didn't, opened or didn't, within X days). A better version would weight different engagement signals (which links, which kind of content, recency curves) and use those to predict who's actually a hot prospect rather than relying on simple boolean rules.
Attribution is a known weak point. The systems we use don't all support UTM tracking end-to-end, which means tying specific revenue back to specific campaigns is harder than it should be. This is partly a tooling problem and partly a process problem, and it's the area where I think the program would benefit most from additional investment.
The Takeaway
Most small marketing teams operate without proper lifecycle infrastructure because building it requires both marketing thinking and technical execution, and most companies don't have anyone who can do both. If you can be that person, you can build systems that meaningfully shift business outcomes (more demos booked, better attribution, healthier email engagement, customers staying longer) at a fraction of the cost of buying enterprise tooling.
The architecture above is what I built for a small B2B company. The same patterns scale up to bigger companies with more sophisticated tools (Braze, Iterable, Customer.io). What stays the same is the underlying logic: capture the lead well, preserve attribution, separate marketing email from transactional email, use behavioral signals to drive segmentation, and treat customers differently from leads.
